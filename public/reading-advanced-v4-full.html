Cooling the concrete city

A

On a summer night, the centre of a large city can be several degrees warmer than the fields that surround it. Scientists call this gap the "urban heat island", and it has grown as cities have spread.

B

The cause is mostly the materials cities are built from. Dark roads and rooftops absorb sunlight during the day and release it slowly after dark, while tall buildings trap the warm air between them.

C

The extra warmth is expensive. Air-conditioning units work harder and for longer, pushing up electricity bills and straining the power grid on the hottest days of the year.

D

The heat is not shared equally. Elderly residents and those with existing illnesses are the most vulnerable, and hospital admissions tend to rise sharply during prolonged hot spells.

E

One simple remedy is to change what sits on top of a building. Painting a roof a pale colour — a so-called "cool roof" — reflects sunlight instead of soaking it up, lowering the temperature inside.

F

Plants help too. Street trees cast shade and release moisture, and gardens planted on rooftops insulate the floors below, so green spaces can cool a neighbourhood as well as brighten it.

G

In one trial, researchers fitted sensors across a district before and after these measures were introduced. They recorded an average fall of three degrees on summer afternoons compared with untreated streets nearby.

H

Convinced by such findings, a growing number of city governments now write cooling targets into their planning rules, requiring reflective surfaces and new planting in major developments.
