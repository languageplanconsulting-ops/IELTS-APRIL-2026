The Value of Urban Green Spaces
Adapted from Cambridge IELTS Academic · Section B

A Urban green spaces — parks, gardens, tree-lined streets — are increasingly recognised not merely as pleasant amenities but as critical infrastructure. Research across dozens of cities demonstrates that access to nature within urban environments is associated with measurable improvements in both mental and physical health outcomes for residents, regardless of socioeconomic background.

B Cities such as Singapore and Copenhagen have pioneered what planners now call the "biophilic city" model, integrating green corridors that connect parks and waterways. The approach ensures that no resident lives more than a ten-minute walk from a significant natural space. Studies tracking residents over five years found that those with consistent green access reported 23% lower rates of anxiety disorders and were more likely to engage in regular physical activity.

C The economic argument is equally compelling. A study conducted by the University of Exeter found that property values within 500 metres of a park were on average 8% higher than comparable properties elsewhere. Moreover, cities with established green networks attracted a greater proportion of skilled workers who cited quality of life as a primary factor in relocation decisions.

D Critics, however, point to the phenomenon of green gentrification — a process whereby park improvements in lower-income neighbourhoods raise property values and subsequently displace the very communities meant to benefit. Urban planners face the challenge of balancing ecological investment with equitable access, ensuring that green upgrades do not inadvertently function as a mechanism of social exclusion.
