Reading Passage 1

The Rise of Digital Nomads

SIZE LINE

A A digital nomad is usually defined as someone who uses digital technologies to work remotely while living or travelling away from a fixed workplace. The idea is not completely new: freelance writers, consultants and programmers were working from laptops long before the term became fashionable. What changed after the pandemic was the scale of remote work and the number of people who realised that a job did not always have to be tied to one city. By 2026, digital nomads are no longer viewed only as adventurous backpackers with blogs. Many are salaried employees, business owners or independent contractors who carry out ordinary professional tasks from temporary homes abroad.

B Governments have responded by creating special visa schemes for remote workers. These visas are intended to regularise a practice that had often existed in a legal grey area, because many people worked online while entering countries as tourists. Most schemes require applicants to show proof of income, health insurance and a contract with a foreign employer or clients outside the host country. The logic is simple: if digital nomads earn money elsewhere but spend it locally, they may support restaurants, transport services, language schools and short-term accommodation without directly competing for local jobs.

C The benefits are not evenly distributed. Cities with beaches, reliable internet and relatively low living costs can attract a sudden flow of higher-earning visitors. Cafes, coworking spaces and apartment owners may welcome the spending, but residents may see rents rise faster than wages. Some critics argue that digital nomads can unintentionally reshape neighbourhoods by increasing demand for furnished flats and international services. Others point out that many nomads stay for only a few months, so their connection with local communities may remain shallow even when their economic impact is visible.

D Employers face a different problem: compliance. If an employee works from another country without permission, the company may create tax, immigration or employment-law obligations without realising it. A worker who spends several months abroad may also handle client data across borders, use unsecured networks or fall outside the insurance arrangements that normally apply at home. For this reason, some firms have introduced formal work-from-anywhere policies. These often limit the number of days employees can spend overseas and require approval before travel begins.

E Digital nomads themselves also describe a mixture of freedom and pressure. They may enjoy choosing where to live, learning languages and avoiding long commutes. However, the lifestyle can involve loneliness, time-zone problems and constant administration. Opening bank accounts, renewing visas and finding quiet places for video meetings can take more energy than social media suggests. Experienced nomads often say that success depends less on travel inspiration than on routine: stable income, careful planning and the ability to work when a new city is more tempting than a laptop screen.

F The rise of digital nomads therefore raises a broader question about the future of work. If jobs can move across borders more easily than people, governments must decide how to tax, welcome and regulate mobile workers. Host cities must also balance visitor income against housing pressure and community change. The digital nomad is sometimes presented as a symbol of personal freedom, but the phenomenon is also a test of institutions designed for a world in which work, residence and citizenship usually belonged in the same place.
